## Three asks (in priority order)

1. **Procedure first.** Ask the council to table any action that creates or vests data-center development rights until a full-buildout impact report is public: electrical load in MW, water source and volumes, cooling technology, generator inventory, and utility will-serve commitments. (Report §37.)
2. **Fix the sound provision.** Nighttime limit in the 45–55 dBA range at the property line or nearest dwelling; add C-/Z-weighted and one-third-octave low-frequency limits; no HVAC or cooling-equipment exemption; buffer width set by acoustic modeling at full buildout, not a round number. (Report §§11–16.)
3. **Put community benefit on the table.** If the project advances, a developer-funded Community Energy Resilience Program scaled per approved MW — efficiency work, household solar + battery backup, community solar for renters, resilience hubs — through a negotiated development agreement with an irrevocable trust. (Report §29.)

## Draft 3-minute public comment

> Mayor Rush, members of the council — thank you for taking up a data-center ordinance before a project is approved. That is the right order, and I want to support making the ordinance strong enough to matter.
>
> Three requests. First, before any approval that gives a developer vested rights, require the basic facts in writing: how many megawatts, where the water comes from and how much, what the cooling technology is, and a utility letter confirming service. Right now none of that is public, and an ordinance written without those facts protects no one.
>
> Second, the reported 70-decibel sound cap is too high. Virginia's own state review found residents losing sleep at 40 to 59 decibels — below limits stricter than this draft. The World Health Organization's nighttime guideline is 40. And an A-weighted number alone misses the low-frequency hum these facilities produce; the ordinance needs low-frequency limits and no exemption for cooling equipment. Council Member Bowden was right to question the number.
>
> Third, if a data center does come, the community should share in the benefit in proportion to the impact. I'd ask the council and the Development Authority to negotiate a developer-funded community energy program — weatherization, solar and battery backup for homes, funded per megawatt through a secured trust — alongside firm requirements that the facility pays its own grid costs and never takes priority over residents' power.
>
> I've prepared a sourced research report on the water, sound, air and grid questions and I'm glad to share it with the council and staff. Thank you.
